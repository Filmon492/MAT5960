\part{Second-order Numerical Methods}
\chapter{A Second-Order Numerical Scheme}\label{chap:chapter_6}
So far, we have considered first-order numerical methods. These methods are robust and can be applied to both the local and nonlocal models. However, their observed order of convergence is at most one. We therefore seek a second-order numerical method to improve computational efficiency.
\section{Semidiscrete Formulation}
The finite volume scheme \eqref{eq: FVM of general f} we stated in Chapter~\ref{chap:chapter_3} is suitable for first order accurate schemes. In order to derive higher-order schemes, we need a formulation which is called a semidiscrete formulation. This formulation allows us to separately increase the order of spatial and temporal accuracy; see \cite[Chapter~5]{Ulrik2015}.

Taking now each cell average over $\mathcal{C}_j$ to be given by
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        u_j(t) = \frac{1}{\Delta x}\int_{x_{j-\sfrac{1}{2}}}^{x_{j+\sfrac{1}{2}}} u(x,t) \,dx
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
and integrating \eqref{eq:local model} over the domain $\mathcal{C}_j$
\begin{align*}
    \int_{x_{j-\sfrac{1}{2}}}^{x_{j+\sfrac{1}{2}}} u_{t} + f(u)_{x} \,dx  = 0
\end{align*}
gives
\[
    \frac{d}{dt}u_j(t) + \sfrac{1}{\Delta x}\Bigl(f(u(x_{j+\sfrac12}^{-},t))- f(u(x_{j-\sfrac12}^{+},t))\Bigr),
\]
where
\[
    u(x_{j+\sfrac12}^{-},t) = \lim_{x\rightarrow x_{j+\sfrac12}^{-}} u(x,t)\]
and
\[
    u(x_{j+\sfrac12}^{+},t) = \lim_{x\rightarrow x_{j+\sfrac12}^{+}} u(x,t)
\]
denotes the left and right-limit of $u$ at $x_{j+\sfrac12}$, respectively. Let $F$ be an approximation of the fluxes such that,
\[
    F_{j+\sfrac12}^{\pm} \approx f(u(x_{j+\sfrac12}^{\pm},t)),
\]
then we obtain the semidiscrete formulation as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:semidiscrete}
    \begin{cases}
        \frac{d}{dt}u_j(t) + \frac{1}{\Delta x}\Bigl(F_{j+\frac12}^{-}- F_{j-\frac12}^{+}\Bigr) = 0 \\[2ex]
        u_j(0)= \frac{1}{\Delta x} \int_{x_{j-\sfrac12}}^{x_{j+\sfrac12}} u_0(x) \, dx
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Which is a system of ODEs that must be integrated in time by a suitable time integration
routine.
\subsection{Second Order Semidiscrete Scheme}
Having the semidiscrete formulation \eqref{eq:semidiscrete}, we can now separately increase
the order of spatial and temporal accuracy. We observe in the formulation above that we need the edge values $u_j^{\pm} = u(x_{j +\sfrac{1}{2}}^\pm,t)$. For those values, we need to construct a linear piecewise function in each cell, so the edge values are evaluated on the reconstructed functions.

Given a cell average $u_j^{n}$ of $\mathcal{C}_j$ at time $t^n$, we can construct a linear function
\[
    \bar{u}_j(x)= u_j^{n} + \sigma_j \frac{x-x_j}{\Delta x}, \quad x \in \mathcal{C}_j
\]
where the slope $\sigma_j$ is selected, such that $\bar{u}_j(x)$ is conservative and satisfy the TVD property. We choose the slope by the minmod limiter
\[
    \sigma_j^n
    =
    minmod\!\left(
    u_j^n-u_{j-1}^n,\, u_{j+1}^n-u_j^n \right),
\]
here the function minmod is defined by
\[
    minmod(a_1,\dots,a_m)
    =
    \begin{cases}
        \operatorname{sgn}(a_1)\min_{1\leq \ell\leq m}|a_\ell|,
           & \text{if } \operatorname{sgn}(a_1)=\cdots=\operatorname{sgn}(a_m), \\[1mm]
        0, & \text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
\]
We can now use $\bar{u}_j$ to approximate $u(x_{j+\sfrac12}^{-},t)$ and $u(x_{j+\sfrac12}^{+},t)$, such that the left and right-limit values are given by
\begin{align*}
    u_{j+\frac12}^-  := \lim_{x\rightarrow x_{j+\sfrac12}^{-}} \bar{u}_j(x)  =u_j^n+\frac12\sigma_j^n
\end{align*}
and
\begin{align*}
    u_{j+\frac12}^+ := \lim_{x\rightarrow x_{j+\sfrac12}^{+}} \bar{u}_j(x) = u_{j+1}^n-\frac12\sigma_{j+1}^n,
\end{align*}
respectively.

In Chapter~\ref{chap:chapter_3}, we approximated the integral term
\begin{equation}\label{eq:integral_term_2}
    \int_{0}^{\epsilon} \omega^{\epsilon}(y) \rho(x+y,t) \, dy
\end{equation}
using piecewise constants, which led us to the first-order finite volume scheme. In order to obtain the second-order finite volume scheme, let $\bar{\rho}_j(x,t)$ be a linear reconstructed function corresponding to the nonlocal model. Then, we approximate the integral term employing the piecewise linear function $\bar{\rho}(x,t)= \bar{\rho}_j(x)$ for $x \in \mathcal{C}_j$ and the trapezoidal rule. Following the approach in \cite{gowdag.d.ConvergenceSecondorderScheme2023},
let
\[M:=\lceil \frac{\epsilon}{\Delta x}\rceil,\qquad \text{denotes the number of cells involved in \eqref{eq:integral_term_2}}, \]
and define
\[
    I_{j+\sfrac12}:= \int_{0}^{\epsilon} \omega^{\epsilon}(y) \rho(x_{j+\sfrac12}+y,t) \, dy.
\]
We apply first $\bar{\rho}$ such that $\rho(x_{j+\sfrac12}+y,t) \approx \bar{\rho}(x_{j+\sfrac12}+y,t)$ to get
\begin{align*}
    I_{j+\sfrac12} & \approx \int_{0}^{\epsilon} \omega^{\epsilon}(y) \bar{\rho}(x_{j+\sfrac12}+y,t) \, dy                         \\
                   & =\sum_{k=0}^{M-1} \int_{k\Delta x}^{(k+1)\Delta x} \omega^{\epsilon}(y) \bar{\rho}(x_{j+\sfrac12}+y,t) \, dy.
\end{align*}
We then apply the trapezoidal rule to get
\begin{align*}
    I_{j+\sfrac12} & \approx \frac{\Delta x}{2}\sum_{k=0}^{M-1} \Bigl(\omega^{\epsilon}(k\Delta x)\bar{\rho}(x_{j+\sfrac12}+k\Delta x,t) + \omega^{\epsilon}((k+1)\Delta x)\bar{\rho}(x_{j+\sfrac12}+(k+1)\Delta x,t)\Bigr) \\
                   & = \frac{\Delta x}{2} \sum_{k=0}^{M-1} \Bigl(\omega^{\epsilon}(k\Delta x)\bar{\rho}(x_{j+k+\sfrac12},t) + \omega^{\epsilon}((k+1)\Delta x)\bar{\rho}(x_{j+k+1+\sfrac12},t) \Bigr)                       \\
                   & = \frac{\Delta x}{2}\sum_{k=0}^{M-1} \Bigl(W_k \bar{\rho}(x_{j+k+\sfrac12},t) + W_{k+1}\bar{\rho}(x_{j+k+\sfrac32},t) \Bigr)                                                                           \\
                   & = \frac{\Delta x}{2}\sum_{k=0}^{M-1} \Bigl( W_k \rho_{j+k+\sfrac12}^+ + W_{k+1}\rho_{j+k+\sfrac32}^- \Bigr)                                                                                            \\
\end{align*}
where $W_k$ is given by
\[ W_k = \omega^{\epsilon}(k\Delta x), \qquad k = 0, \cdots M.\]
We define the numerical quadrature $\tilde{W}_k$
\[
    Q_{\Delta x}:= \frac{\Delta x}{2} \sum_{k=0}^{M-1} \Bigl( W_k + W_{k+1}\Bigr), \quad \text{ and } \tilde{W}_k:= \frac{W_k}{Q_{\Delta x}}, \qquad k = 0, \cdots M,
\]
such that the normalization condition is satisfied, in the sense
\[
    \frac{\Delta x}{2} \sum_{k=0}^{M-1} \Bigl( \tilde{W}_k + \tilde{W}_{k+1}\Bigr) = 1.
\]
If we now replace $W_k$ by $\tilde{W}_k$ in
\[
    R_{j+\sfrac12}:= \frac{\Delta x}{2} \sum_{k=0}^{M-1} \Bigl( W_k \rho_{j+k+\sfrac12}^+ + W_{k+1}\rho_{j+k+\sfrac32}^- \Bigr),
\]
we obtain
\[
    R_{j+\sfrac12}= \frac{\Delta x}{2} \sum_{k=0}^{M-1} \Bigl( \tilde{W}_k \rho_{j+k+\sfrac12}^+ + \tilde{W}_{k+1}\rho_{j+k+\sfrac32}^- \Bigr).
\]
Now let the numerical flux $F_{j+\sfrac12}^-$ in \eqref{eq:semidiscrete} be defined by the Godunov-type flux
\[
    F_{j+\sfrac12}^- := \rho_{j+\sfrac12}^-(1-R_{j+\sfrac12}),
\]
and denote
\[
    L_{\epsilon}(\rho)_j = \frac{1}{\Delta x} ( F_{j+\sfrac12}^- - F_{j-\sfrac12}^+ ).
\]
Then, we obtain the nonlocal semidiscrete scheme:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:nonlocal-semidiscrete}
    \begin{cases}
        \displaystyle
        \frac{d}{dt} \rho_j(t) + L_{\epsilon}(\rho)_j = 0 \\[2ex]
        \displaystyle
        \rho_j(0)= \frac{1}{\Delta x} \int_{x_{j-\sfrac12}}^{x_{j+\sfrac12}} \rho_0(x) \, dx
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
\begin{proposition}\label{pro:positive_result}
    Suppose $\omega^{\epsilon}$ has compact support in $[0,\epsilon]$. Let $\Delta x > 0$ be fixed. Then, as $\epsilon \to 0$, the nonlocal semidiscrete scheme
    \eqref{eq:nonlocal-semidiscrete} converges to the local semidiscrete scheme given by
    \begin{equation}\label{eq:local-semidiscrete}
        \begin{cases}
            \displaystyle
            \frac{d}{dt} u_j(t) + L(u)_j = 0, \\[2ex]
            \displaystyle
            u_j(0) = \frac{1}{\Delta x} \int_{x_{j-\sfrac12}}^{x_{j+\sfrac12}} u_0(x)\, dx,
        \end{cases}
    \end{equation}
    where
    \[
        L(u)_j := \frac{1}{\Delta x}
        \bigl(G_{j+\sfrac12}^- - G_{j-\sfrac12}^+ \bigr) ,\qquad G_{j+\sfrac12}^- := u_{j+\sfrac12}^-(1 - u_{j+\sfrac12}^+)
    \]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
    It suffices to prove that
    \[
        R_{j+\frac12} \to \rho_{j+\frac12}^+
        \qquad \text{as } \epsilon \to 0,
    \]
    since then we get
    \[
        F_{j+\frac12}^- = \rho_{j+\sfrac12}^-(1 - \rho_{j+\sfrac12}^+),
    \]
    which has the same form as
    \[
        G_{j+\sfrac12}^- = u_{j+\sfrac12}^-(1 - u_{j+\sfrac12}^+).
    \]
    We keep the convention that \(u\) denotes the local solution and \(\rho\) denotes the nonlocal solution. If $\Delta x$ is fixed, and $\epsilon \rightarrow 0$, soon or later we get $ \epsilon < \Delta x$. In that case $M$ = 1. Hence we get
    \[Q_{\Delta x} = \frac{\Delta x}{2} \Bigl(W_0 + W_{1}\Bigr), \qquad \tilde{W}_0= \frac{W_0}{Q_{\Delta x}} , \qquad \tilde{W}_1= \frac{W_1}{Q_{\Delta x}},\]
    and
    \[ R_{j+\sfrac12} = \frac{\Delta x}{2} \Bigl( \tilde{W}_0 \rho_{j+\sfrac12}^+ + \tilde{W}_{1}\rho_{j+\sfrac32}^- \Bigr). \]
    Since $\omega^{\epsilon}$ has compact support in $[0,\epsilon]$, we have
    \[ W_1= \omega^{\epsilon}(\Delta x) = 0  \text{ and } \tilde{W}_1 = 0.\]
    So
    \[ R_{j+\sfrac12} = \frac{\Delta x}{2} \tilde{W}_0 \rho_{j+\sfrac12}^+, \]
    and
    \[Q_{\Delta x} = \frac{\Delta x}{2}W_0 , \qquad \tilde{W}_0= \frac{W_0}{Q_{\Delta x}} = \frac{2}{\Delta x}.\]
    Hence
    \[
        R_{j+\frac12} = \rho_{j+\frac12}^+.
    \]
    Therefore, \(F_{j+\sfrac12}^- \to G_{j+\sfrac12}^-\) and
    \[
        L_\epsilon(\rho)_j \to L(u)_j
        \qquad \text{as } \epsilon \to 0.
    \]
    This proves the claim.
\end{proof}
\subsection{Second Order Godunov Scheme}
Let us apply now the forward Euler method to the nonlocal semidiscrete \eqref{eq:nonlocal-semidiscrete} to obtain
\[
    \rho_{j}^{n+1} = \rho_{j}^n - \Delta t L_{\epsilon}(\rho^n)_j.
\]
It turns out this scheme is only first-order accurate, even though we have used the piecewise linear reconstruction in space; see \cite[p.~76]{Ulrik2015}. In order to obtain a second-order accurate scheme, we have to use the so-called strong stability preserving (SSP) Runge-Kutta methods. Hence we need to perform the following steps
\begin{equation}\label{eq:SSP-Godunov}
    \begin{split}
        \rho_{j}^{(1)} & =  \rho_{j}^n  - \Delta t L_{\epsilon}(\rho^n)_j           \\
        \rho_{j}^{(2)} & =   \rho_{j}^{(1)}   - \Delta t L_{\epsilon}(\rho^{(1)})_j \\
        \rho_{j}^{n+1} & = \frac{1}{2}(\rho_{j}^n +  \rho_{j}^{(2)})
    \end{split}
\end{equation}
to obtain the second-order Godunov scheme.

\section{Conclusion}

The second-order numerical method for the nonlocal model \eqref{eq:nonlocal_model} was developed in \cite{gowdag.d.ConvergenceSecondorderScheme2023}. However, the authors do not address asymptotic compatibility. Moreover, their BV estimate in space blow up as \(\Delta x \to 0\) and \(\epsilon \to 0\). This motivates our investigation of the asymptotic compatibility problem.

Our first positive result was the convergence of the nonlocal-to-local limit, summarized in Proposition~\ref{pro:positive_result}. To prove asymptotic compatibility, it may be useful to investigate whether the second-order scheme satisfies a one-sided Lipschitz condition analogous to that established for first-order schemes in Lemma~\ref{lem:lemma_1}.
